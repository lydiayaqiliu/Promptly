Levels of Analysis in Psychological Science: Memory, Emotion, and the Case for Multi-Level Explanation
One of the central ambitions of psychological science is not merely to describe what people do, but to explain why and how they do it. These explanations — mechanisms — can operate at biological, psychological, or social levels of analysis, and no single level tells the whole story. This essay examines two psychological phenomena: (1) the consolidation of long-term memory, explained primarily at the neural level, and (2) the experience of emotion, explained primarily at the psychological level through cognitive appraisal theory. For each, I argue that enriching our understanding requires reaching across levels of analysis — and that this cross-level ambition reflects something fundamental about what good scientific explanation looks like.
Phenomenon 1: Memory Consolidation (Neural Level)
Memory consolidation refers to the process by which newly acquired information is stabilized into long-term memory. The dominant mechanistic account operates at the biological level. During and after learning, the hippocampus plays a critical role in binding together the distributed cortical representations that constitute a memory trace. Over time — and especially during sleep — the hippocampus "replays" recent experiences, gradually transferring representations to the neocortex for long-term storage (Squire & Alvarez, 1995). This systems-level consolidation is complemented by synaptic consolidation, in which molecular changes at individual synapses — including protein synthesis and the strengthening of synaptic connections via long-term potentiation (LTP) — stabilize the neural substrate of memory (McGaugh, 2000).
The evidence for these neural mechanisms is compelling. Lesion studies of patients like H.M., who had his hippocampus surgically removed and subsequently lost the ability to form new declarative memories, demonstrated the hippocampus's necessity for consolidation (Scoville & Milner, 1957). Neuroimaging studies have further confirmed hippocampal activation during encoding and sleep-dependent reactivation. Animal studies have shown that blocking protein synthesis in the hours after learning disrupts long-term memory formation, underscoring the role of synaptic-level biochemistry (McGaugh, 2000).
Yet a purely neural account leaves important questions unanswered. Why, for instance, are emotionally significant or socially relevant events remembered so much better than neutral ones? Understanding this requires a psychological level of analysis. Research on the emotion-memory interaction suggests that arousal at the time of encoding — mediated partly by norepinephrine release in the amygdala — enhances hippocampal consolidation (McGaugh, 2000). But the meaning of an event, not just its arousal value, determines its memorability. A psychological account invoking elaborative encoding, self-referential processing, and motivated attention could explain why people remember events that matter to their goals, relationships, and self-concept far better than events that don't. Testing this would require moving from the neural substrate to cognitive constructs such as schema activation and attentional prioritization. In doing so, we would gain a richer picture of memory consolidation — not just as a synaptic event, but as a process shaped by the psychological significance of experience.
Phenomenon 2: Emotion and Cognitive Appraisal (Psychological Level)
The second phenomenon is the subjective experience of emotion, and the primary mechanistic account here is cognitive appraisal theory. Developed by Lazarus (1991) and others, appraisal theory holds that emotions are not automatic responses to stimuli but arise from the evaluation of events in relation to one's goals, coping resources, and well-being. An event appraised as a threat produces fear; the same event appraised as a challenge may produce excitement. Crucially, it is the appraisal — not the event itself — that determines the emotional response. This is a psychological-level mechanism: the explanation is framed in terms of cognitive processes (evaluation, comparison, inference) rather than neurons or social structures.
The evidence for appraisal theory is robust. Classic studies by Schachter and Singer (1962) demonstrated that the same state of physiological arousal could be experienced as euphoria or anger depending on contextual cues and cognitive interpretation, suggesting that meaning-making, not mere physiology, drives emotional experience. Cross-cultural research has supported the universality of certain appraisal dimensions (e.g., goal relevance, agency) while also showing variation in how cultures define and interpret emotional situations (Ellsworth & Scherer, 2003). Experimental studies in which researchers manipulate appraisals — for example, by reframing a stressful task as an opportunity rather than a threat — reliably alter both subjective emotion and physiological responses, further validating the causal role of appraisal.
However, a psychological account alone cannot explain important patterns in emotional experience. Why do individuals differ so dramatically in their default appraisal tendencies? Some people habitually appraise ambiguous situations as threatening; others appraise the same situations as benign. A biological level of analysis could illuminate this. Research on the genetic heritability of neuroticism — a trait associated with negative appraisal biases — suggests that individual differences in appraisal style have partly heritable origins (Bouchard & McGue, 2003). At the neural level, amygdala reactivity, prefrontal regulation, and the serotonin transporter gene (5-HTTLPR) have all been implicated in shaping emotional sensitivity and appraisal tendencies. Incorporating this biological dimension would allow researchers to understand not just how appraisals produce emotions in the moment, but why some individuals are constitutionally predisposed to appraise the world in particular ways — bridging the gap between neural architecture and lived emotional experience.
The Larger Argument: Why Levels of Analysis Matter
These two examples illustrate a broader principle about scientific explanation: no single level of analysis is sufficient on its own. Neural accounts of memory consolidation are powerful, but they cannot explain why meaning and emotion shape what we remember without invoking psychological constructs. Psychological accounts of emotion are compelling, but they cannot explain individual differences in appraisal without recourse to biology. As Marr (1982) classically argued in the context of vision science, understanding a system fully requires explanation at multiple levels — the computational (what is being done and why), the algorithmic (how it is done), and the implementational (in what physical substrate). Psychological science is richer when these levels speak to one another.
The phenomena examined here are not exceptions but illustrations of a rule: the most complete scientific explanations are multi-level ones. Memory is both a synaptic event and a psychologically meaningful act of interpretation. Emotion is both a neural response and a cognitive construction shaped by personal history. To commit too rigidly to any single level of analysis is to mistake a partial map for the territory itself.
