Levels of Analysis and Mechanisms of Stress: From Genes to Social Hierarchies
Introduction
One of the central insights of the Mechanisms of Mind course is that psychological and behavioral phenomena cannot be fully understood through any single level of analysis. Biological, psychological, and social mechanisms are not competing explanations for the same thing — they are complementary lenses that illuminate different facets of the same phenomenon. A complete scientific explanation requires what the course framed as multi-level understanding: every psychological theory should have a biological correlate, every biological finding should be interpretable at a psychological level, and neither level is more "correct" than the other (Norman, 2026). This paper examines two phenomena drawn from the course — differential susceptibility to stress based on serotonin transporter genotype, and the stress physiology of social dominance in male baboons — and argues that each is currently explained primarily at one level of analysis but would be substantially enriched by investigation at another. Together, these examples illustrate a broader claim: that the explanatory power of any mechanistic account depends not on its level of description alone, but on how that account is connected to mechanisms operating above and below it.
Phenomenon 1: Differential Susceptibility and the 5-HTTLPR Polymorphism
The Phenomenon and Its Biological Mechanism
The first phenomenon concerns why individuals exposed to the same stressful life events show radically different behavioral and psychological outcomes. Covault et al. (2007) examined this question in a longitudinal study of college students, measuring daily drinking and drug use across two academic years while tracking participants' self-reported negative life events and genotyping them at the serotonin transporter linked promoter region (5-HTTLPR). This polymorphism involves a functional variation in the promoter region of the gene encoding the serotonin transporter (5-HTT), which regulates the reuptake of serotonin following synaptic release. Individuals carrying two short (s) alleles show reduced transcriptional activity of the gene compared to those carrying long (l) alleles, resulting in lower serotonin transporter availability.
Covault et al. found a significant gene-by-environment interaction: students homozygous for the s allele who experienced more past-year negative life events reported substantially higher rates of drinking, heavy drinking, drinking intentions, and nonprescribed drug use compared to their peers with the l/l genotype. For students with l/l genotypes, stressful life events were essentially unrelated to substance use. The s/s students who experienced the most stress drank on 38–53% of sampled days across the two years, compared to roughly 20–25% for the full sample. The effect for drug use was especially striking: s/s students reporting many stressors used nonprescribed drugs on approximately 40% of sampled days, while drug use was rare and stress-independent among l/l students.
This finding connects directly to Belsky et al.'s (2009) differential susceptibility framework, which challenges the traditional diathesis-stress model. Where the diathesis-stress model treats the s allele as a vulnerability gene — one that increases risk for pathological outcomes under adversity — Belsky et al. propose that such genes are better understood as plasticity genes. Carriers of the s allele are not simply more vulnerable to harm; they are more sensitive to environmental inputs generally, for better and for worse. In supportive or enriched environments, s allele carriers often show the best outcomes of any genotype group; in adverse environments, they show the worst. The biological mechanism underlying this plasticity is rooted in serotonergic signaling: because the short allele reduces serotonin reuptake efficiency, it may produce a system that is more reactive to environmental variation in ways that shape learning, emotional processing, and behavioral regulation.
Evidence Supporting the Biological Account
The evidence for this mechanism is reasonably strong. Covault et al.'s (2007) findings replicated across two consecutive academic years, lending confidence to the reliability of the interaction. The gene-by-environment interaction was consistent across multiple dependent variables — drinking frequency, heavy drinking frequency, drinking intentions, and drug use — and remained significant when controlling for sex, neuroticism, and sensation seeking. Crucially, genotype was unrelated to negative life events, ruling out simple gene-environment correlation as an alternative explanation. Belsky et al. (2009) review additional replication studies showing that the s allele modifies responses to both adversity and support across outcomes including depression, anxiety, and ADHD symptoms. The biological plausibility of the mechanism is also established: the 5-HTTLPR polymorphism has documented functional consequences for serotonin transporter mRNA expression and, as Sapolsky (2015) notes, the serotonin transporter gene has become a landmark example in understanding gene-by-environment interactions in psychiatric genetics.
Nonetheless, important limitations remain. Covault et al.'s sample was restricted to non-Hispanic Caucasians at a single university, limiting generalizability. Effect sizes, while consistent, were modest in magnitude — typically accounting for 1–4% of variance. And the biological mechanism relating 5-HTTLPR to behavior remains incompletely specified: why does reduced serotonin reuptake translate specifically into stress-reactive substance use rather than, say, stress-reactive social withdrawal or anxiety?
How a Psychological-Level Analysis Would Enrich Understanding
The biological account tells us that s allele carriers are more environmentally sensitive, and that serotonergic variation is involved. But it does not tell us how the environment gets "inside" the person to produce differential behavioral outcomes. This is precisely where a psychological-level mechanism is needed.
The course introduced appraisal theory as a key psychological mechanism linking stressors to stress responses (Norman, 2026). Primary appraisal refers to the process by which individuals evaluate whether a situation is threatening, safe, or irrelevant to their goals. Secondary appraisal refers to the assessment of what resources are available to cope with the stressor. When individuals perceive demands as exceeding their coping resources, the result is a threat appraisal, which is associated with passive coping strategies and a vascular rather than cardiac stress response profile. When resources are perceived as sufficient, the result is a challenge appraisal, associated with active coping and a more adaptive physiological profile.
A psychological-level investigation of 5-HTTLPR would ask whether s allele carriers systematically differ in their appraisal processes when confronted with stressors. Do s/s individuals appraise a given negative life event as more threatening — that is, as exceeding their available coping resources — compared to l/l individuals experiencing the same event? If so, does this threat appraisal mediate the relationship between stress exposure and substance use? The course also discussed coping strategies directly: active coping involves directly addressing the source of a stressor, while passive coping involves avoidance or indirect emotional regulation. Substance use is a canonical example of passive coping. If s allele carriers are more likely to appraise stressors as threats and consequently shift toward passive coping, this would provide a psychological-level mechanism linking serotonergic sensitivity to stress-reactive drinking.
This kind of analysis is not mere speculation — it is grounded in the course's broader framework for levels of explanation. As Norman (2026) emphasized, biological and psychological descriptions are not mutually exclusive. Every biological finding constrains and is constrained by psychological theory. The inverted-U model discussed by Sapolsky (2015) makes this point clearly: the same biological system (the glucocorticoid receptor dual-system involving mineralocorticoid and glucocorticoid receptors) produces nonlinear behavioral outcomes that can only be understood by connecting neurobiological mechanism to behavioral endpoint. Similarly, understanding why 5-HTTLPR genotype produces stress-reactive substance use requires connecting the serotonergic mechanism to the psychological processes of appraisal, coping strategy selection, and behavioral regulation.
Phenomenon 2: Social Dominance and Stress Physiology in Male Baboons
The Phenomenon and Its Biological Mechanism
The second phenomenon concerns the relationship between social rank and stress physiology. The prevailing hypothesis in behavioral endocrinology had long been that high social rank is physiologically advantageous — dominant animals enjoy lower glucocorticoid levels and thus less chronic stress. This was understood as reflecting the benefits of control, predictability, and resource access that come with high status. However, Gesquiere et al. (2011) challenged a simple version of this account in a landmark nine-year study of wild male savannah baboons (Papio cynocephalus) in Amboseli, Kenya.
Analyzing over 2,400 monthly hormone values from 125 adult males across five social groups, Gesquiere et al. found that while fecal glucocorticoid (fGC) concentrations generally declined with increasing rank — suggesting that higher rank is indeed associated with lower stress — alpha males (the highest-ranking individuals) were a striking exception. Alpha males showed fGC levels comparable to males near the bottom of the hierarchy, significantly higher than beta males (second-ranked) and higher than males ranked 2 through 8. This nonlinear, non-monotonic relationship was present regardless of whether the dominance hierarchy was stable or unstable, challenging earlier hypotheses that the costs of dominance only emerge during periods of instability. Testosterone followed a straightforwardly linear pattern — higher rank predicted higher testosterone — suggesting that the glucocorticoid elevations in alpha males were not simply a function of reproductive activity suppressing the stress axis.
The biological mechanism proposed by Gesquiere et al. is primarily energetic. Alpha males in Amboseli experienced 17% higher rates of agonistic encounters and spent 29% more time in sexual consortships than beta males. Both activities are metabolically costly and activate hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis signaling. The HPA axis, as described extensively in the course, operates through a cascade: perceived stressors activate the amygdala, which stimulates the hypothalamus to release corticotropin releasing hormone (CRH), which in turn signals the anterior pituitary to release adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH), which finally triggers cortisol (or in non-human primates, equivalent glucocorticoids) release from the adrenal cortex (Norman, 2026). Chronically elevated glucocorticoids produce wide-ranging effects across bodily systems, including immunosuppression, cardiovascular strain, and structural changes to the hippocampus and prefrontal cortex. The implication is that the physiological costs of maintaining alpha status — defending rank against challengers and guarding reproductive access to fertile females — produce a chronic HPA activation that erodes the health advantages one might expect dominance to confer.
Evidence Supporting the Biological Account
The evidence base for this finding is unusually strong for behavioral endocrinology. The nine-year longitudinal design spanning 125 males across five social groups provides statistical power rarely achievable in studies of wild primate populations. The within-individual comparison — tracking individuals who occupied both alpha and beta positions at different times — provides a particularly compelling test, as it controls for stable individual differences that might otherwise confound rank comparisons. The alpha-versus-beta difference in fGC levels held in both stable and unstable hierarchies, which is a key finding because it rules out the possibility that alpha stress is simply an artifact of rank instability. Furthermore, the proposed mechanism is specific and testable: the investigators were able to measure agonistic encounter rates and consortship time directly and show that these behavioral costs were higher for alpha than beta males, even as rates of challenges received and grooming received were similar between the two groups.
Sapolsky's (2015) commentary situates this finding within the broader framework of the inverted-U relationship between stress and outcomes. The course discussed how both the absence of stress and severe chronic stress are harmful, while moderate stress is often adaptive. Alpha males may represent a case of chronic overstimulation — pushed beyond the optimal point on the inverted-U — not because of subordination-related unpredictability and lack of control, but because of the sheer metabolic and competitive demands of occupying the top position. As the course also discussed, controllability and predictability are among the most powerful determinants of how stressful a situation is: a stressor that can be predicted or controlled produces substantially less physiological stress response than one that cannot (Norman, 2026). Alpha males, despite high control over resources, may face highly unpredictable challenges to their position, given that their tenure is typically short and challengers are persistent.
How a Social-Level Analysis Would Enrich Understanding
The biological account explains the proximate mechanism linking high rank to elevated glucocorticoids through energetic costs. But it leaves open a crucial question: why is being at the very top so specifically costly compared to being second? The difference in agonistic encounter rates between alpha and beta males was only 17% — substantial but not overwhelming — and yet the fGC difference between them was large and robust. A purely metabolic account may be insufficient.
This is where a social-level analysis would substantially enrich understanding. The course discussed at length how social factors shape stress responses through the processes of appraisal and perceived threat (Norman, 2026). Social dominance is not a fixed physiological state; it is a continuously negotiated social relationship. Alpha males occupy a position that carries uniquely high social visibility and uniquely high social stakes: every interaction with every other male carries the possibility of a status-relevant challenge. The alpha position may produce a chronic state of social vigilance that has no parallel even one rank below.
The course also introduced the concept of loneliness and the need to belong as fundamental drivers of social stress in humans, noting that social threats are among the most powerful and health-relevant stressors that socially interdependent species face (Norman, 2026). An analogous framework may apply to baboons: the alpha male may be in a state of chronic social isolation — surrounded by potential rivals rather than allies — that produces psychological stress over and above the energetic demands of rank maintenance. Gesquiere et al. noted that alpha and beta males received similar rates of grooming from females, suggesting that social buffering is not differentially available to them. But the quality and character of those social bonds — whether they function as genuine sources of comfort and affiliation versus mere proximity — would require social-level analysis to assess.
From a human perspective, the course discussed how social rank and social integration together determine health outcomes, with social relationships showing larger effect sizes on mortality than smoking, physical activity, or obesity (Norman, 2026). A social-level analysis of the baboon data would investigate whether alpha males differ in the nature of their social networks: do they have fewer reciprocal affiliative relationships? Do they experience more social uncertainty — not knowing whether any given approach is friendly or threatening? Such analysis would connect the biological findings to the social mechanisms that the allostatic model of stress identifies as key inputs to perceived stress, operating alongside chronic social stressors, major life stressors, and physiological stressors to shape behavioral and physiological responses (Norman, 2026).
Broader Argument: Why Levels of Analysis Cannot Be Collapsed
These two phenomena together illustrate a general principle about scientific explanation that the course articulated from the outset: levels of analysis are not hierarchically ordered such that one is more fundamental or more correct than the others. They address different questions. As Norman (2026) emphasized, the reductionist claim that neural or genetic explanations are superior because they are more "biological" misunderstands the nature of explanation. A gene does not cause a behavior; it produces a protein that participates in a system that is embedded in a developmental history, in a social environment, and in a body that is being asked to respond to specific demands. Each of these levels of embedding is causally relevant.
In the case of 5-HTTLPR, the biological mechanism (reduced serotonin reuptake) constrains but does not determine the behavioral outcome. Whether the heightened environmental sensitivity of s allele carriers manifests as stress-reactive drinking, heightened empathy, or superior responsiveness to supportive parenting depends on what the environment demands and what psychological resources the individual has available. A biological explanation that ignores appraisal processes and coping strategies cannot predict which adaptive or maladaptive outcome will emerge. In the baboon case, the biological finding (elevated glucocorticoids in alpha males) is robust, but the social mechanism that makes the alpha position uniquely stressful — not just energetically costly but socially precarious in a distinctive way — requires a social-level account to explain the specificity of the effect.
Sapolsky (2015) makes this point directly in arguing that individual differences in stress responsiveness — the question of what constitutes stress "for this individual" — is the most important topic in stress neurobiology. Answering that question requires knowing not just an individual's genotype or neuroendocrine profile, but their learning history, their social position, their appraisal tendencies, and the cultural context in which they are embedded. This is precisely the multi-level integrative project that the course has pursued throughout the quarter.
Conclusion
Differential susceptibility and social dominance stress physiology each demonstrate that phenomena studied primarily at one level of analysis carry important explanatory gaps that can only be filled by mechanisms operating at other levels. The 5-HTTLPR finding is biologically grounded but requires psychological-level accounts of appraisal and coping to explain how genetic sensitivity is translated into specific behavioral outcomes. The baboon dominance finding is biologically and behaviorally documented but requires social-level analysis of alliance quality, social vigilance, and the unique precariousness of the alpha position to explain why the very top of a hierarchy is more costly than the position just below it. In both cases, the richer explanation is not the one that goes deepest into the biology, but the one that most clearly specifies how mechanisms at each level interact to produce the phenomenon in question.
