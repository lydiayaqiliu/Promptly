Beyond the Given: How and Why the Mind Constructs Reality
The raw data streaming into our senses at any given moment is radically incomplete — a flat retinal image, a pressure wave in the ear, a chemical signal on the tongue. Yet what we experience is a rich, stable, meaningful world populated by objects, intentions, causes, and consequences. This gap between sensory input and conscious experience is not a bug in the system; it is the system. The central claim of this paper is that minds go beyond the information given through both genetically prepared (closed) programs and experience-dependent (open) programs, and that this constructive capacity exists because the challenges of survival and social living demand predictions, not merely recordings. The mind is not a camera — it is a hypothesis engine, and understanding both its mechanisms and its evolutionary rationale reveals why reality, though shared in broad strokes, is always partly a personal construction.
What Is "the Information Given," and What Does It Mean to Go Beyond It?
"The information given" refers to the raw, uninterpreted signals arriving at our sensory receptors: photons hitting the retina, air pressure oscillations vibrating the cochlea, mechanical force on the skin. Going "beyond" this information means that our perception, cognition, and social understanding add structure, meaning, causality, and prediction that are not contained in the signal itself. The mind fills gaps, imposes categories, infers hidden states, and anticipates futures — all without conscious deliberation. Jerome Bruner, whose phrase titles this paper, argued that all cognition is fundamentally an act of categorization and inference (Bruner, 1973). The question for cognitive science is how these additions are made and why the mind is built to make them.
HOW: Closed Programs and the Architecture of Prepared Perception
Some of the most striking examples of going beyond the given are built directly into the nervous system — what we might call closed programs: genetically canalized processes that require minimal environmental input to develop and that operate largely automatically. Visual perception offers the clearest illustration. The visual system does not passively receive an image; it actively constructs one. The phenomenon of perceptual completion — filling in the blind spot, completing occluded contours, perceiving illusory figures like the Kanizsa triangle — demonstrates that the brain is constantly generating predictions about what should be there based on surrounding context, even when that region of space provides no signal at all (Lecture 2, slide 14). The brain adds information that is not in the retinal image.
Depth perception provides another example. The retina is two-dimensional, yet we perceive a three-dimensional world. To accomplish this, the visual system uses cues — binocular disparity, motion parallax, relative size, interposition — to compute depth. These computations go far beyond the given; they require the brain to solve an inverse problem, inferring the three-dimensional cause of a two-dimensional effect (Marr, 1982, as cited in Lecture 2). Crucially, many of these mechanisms are cross-culturally robust and appear early in development, suggesting a strong innate component. Infant studies using the visual cliff paradigm (Gibson & Walk, 1960, as cited in Lecture 3) show that even very young infants who have had minimal experience respond to apparent depth, indicating that the perceptual system comes prepared to make certain inferences.
Categorical perception of phonemes offers a bridge between perception and language. Acoustic speech signals exist on a continuum, yet listeners perceive sharp categorical boundaries — a phenomenon known as categorical perception. The boundary between /b/ and /p/, for instance, is perceived as a sudden shift even though voice onset time varies continuously (Lecture 4, slide 8; Liberman et al., 1957). The mind imposes discrete categories on a continuous signal, going beyond what is literally given in the sound wave. This capacity appears to have a partially innate basis, as neonates show sensitivity to phoneme-relevant distinctions across languages, though experience subsequently narrows perception toward the native language (Werker & Tees, 1984, as cited in Lecture 4). Here we see closed and open programs interacting: an innate readiness to categorize speech sounds is subsequently sculpted by linguistic experience.
HOW: Open Programs and the Role of Experience
While closed programs establish default inferences, open programs allow the mind's constructions to be shaped by individual experience, culture, and learning. Memory is perhaps the most powerful example. Bartlett's (1932) classic study of "War of the Ghosts" showed that when participants recalled an unfamiliar Native American folktale, they systematically distorted the story in the direction of their own cultural schemas — omitting strange details, rationalizing ambiguous passages, and introducing familiar elements. Memory, Bartlett concluded, is not reproductive but reconstructive: we go beyond what was given at encoding by using our existing knowledge structures to fill in and regularize what we store (Lecture 6, slide 5). The information given at encoding is transformed into something shaped by everything the mind already knows.
This reconstructive quality of memory has profound consequences. The misinformation effect, demonstrated by Loftus and Palmer (1974, as cited in Lecture 6), shows that post-event suggestions can alter what witnesses report remembering — changing "smashed" to "hit" in a question about a car accident altered speed estimates and false memories of broken glass. The mind goes beyond the original sensory record by integrating subsequent information, literally rewriting the past. What this reveals is that memory is not a replay mechanism but an inference mechanism: the mind reconstructs the most probable past from fragmentary traces and current knowledge.
Conceptual categorization illustrates how open programs shape the very concepts through which we interpret experience. Rosch's (1975, as cited in Lecture 5) prototype theory showed that category membership is graded, not all-or-nothing — a robin is a "better" bird than a penguin. Categories are not logical definitions but fuzzy clusters around prototypes, shaped by experience with exemplars. This means that what counts as an instance of a concept — a chair, a vehicle, a crime — depends on culturally and individually variable experience. Two people sharing the same physical environment may literally categorize and therefore perceive it differently, demonstrating that even at the level of basic object recognition, the mind adds structure that is not in the sensory signal.
WHY: The Functional Imperative of Prediction
Understanding the mechanisms of construction raises the deeper question: why is the mind built this way? Why not simply process what is given, faithfully and without addition? The answer lies in the challenges that shaped the evolution and development of mind. The core argument is that prediction is more adaptive than recording. An organism that merely registers the present is always behind; an organism that predicts the future can act in advance.
Consider the evolutionary challenge of social living. Primates, and humans especially, live in complex social groups where survival depends on anticipating the behavior of conspecifics — predicting who will cooperate, who will defect, who is telling the truth. This challenge gave rise to what Premack and Woodruff (1978, as cited in Lecture 8) called a "theory of mind": the capacity to attribute mental states — beliefs, desires, intentions — to others. Theory of mind is a powerful example of going beyond the given, because mental states are by definition not observable. We infer them from behavior, context, and our own experience of having minds. The false belief task (Wimmer & Perner, 1983, as cited in Lecture 8) demonstrates that by around age four, children understand that others can hold beliefs that differ from reality — they go beyond the observable scene to model another person's epistemic state. The function is clear: modeling others' minds allows for cooperation, deception detection, teaching, and the complex coordination of human social life.
Causal reasoning similarly goes beyond the given in service of prediction and control. Michotte's (1946, as cited in Lecture 7) launching experiments showed that humans perceive causality directly and automatically — we see one ball cause another to move, even though causality itself is not a sensory property but an inference. Hume famously noted that we observe only constant conjunction, never causation itself, yet the mind adds causal structure because causal models are immensely useful: they allow us to predict future events and to reason counterfactually about how to intervene in the world. The function of causal going-beyond is control.
The challenge of operating in a noisy, ambiguous environment also explains why the mind uses priors — prior probabilities — to disambiguate sensation. Bayesian frameworks describe perception as the combination of sensory evidence with prior expectations (Lecture 2, slide 21; Weiss, Simoncelli & Adelson, 2002). When sensory evidence is weak or ambiguous, priors dominate, and we perceive what we expect rather than what is given. This is precisely why illusions occur and why expectations shape experience: the brain is solving a statistical inference problem, and sometimes the prior is wrong. But in a world where most input is consistent with prior experience, this system is extraordinarily efficient. The why is efficiency under uncertainty.
Commonality and Divergence: The Shared and Personal Construction
To what extent do individuals share the same constructed reality? The answer is: substantially, but not completely. Closed programs produce commonalities — all neurologically typical humans share the Kanizsa illusion, categorical phoneme perception, and intuitive causal inference, because these constructions are built on shared neural architecture shaped by common evolutionary history. Language and culture produce further commonalities within groups: shared schemas, shared prototypes, shared narratives structure perception and memory similarly for people within a cultural community. This explains why communication is possible — our constructions are correlated enough to coordinate action.
Yet individual experience, developmental history, and current motivational state all introduce divergence. The same ambiguous figure — the Necker cube, the duck-rabbit — can be seen in genuinely different ways by different observers, or by the same observer at different times. Memory is personally reconstructed using individual schemas. Emotional state influences what is perceived, remembered, and inferred (Lecture 9; Bower, 1981, on mood-congruent memory). The constructed nature of reality means that no two people inhabit exactly the same experiential world, and the same person's world changes over time as their priors, schemas, and knowledge change.
Conclusion
The mind goes beyond the information given through a layered architecture of closed programs that impose prepared inferences on sensory data and open programs that sculpt these constructions through experience. It does so because the challenges of surviving and navigating a complex, social, causally structured world demand not a faithful record of the present but a useful model of the past and a prediction of the future. Perception, memory, categorization, social cognition, and causal reasoning are all, at their core, acts of inference that add to the given. This constructive capacity is what makes minds powerful — and what makes experience, inevitably and irreducibly, partly a creation of the mind itself.
