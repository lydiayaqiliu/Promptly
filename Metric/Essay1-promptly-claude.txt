Constructing Reality: How and Why the Mind Goes Beyond the Information Given
The raw sensory data available to an organism at any given moment — the unprocessed signals arriving at receptor surfaces — constitute what Bruner (1957) termed "the information given." Yet human cognition does not passively receive this input. It actively interprets, supplements, and transforms it: imposing structure on ambiguous stimuli, filling gaps that sensory data leave open, generating predictions, and organizing experience through learned categories and social schemas. This paper argues that the mind goes beyond the information given through the dynamic interplay of closed programs — evolutionarily canalized, relatively automatic mechanisms — and open programs — experience-dependent, flexible mechanisms shaped by development and culture (Mayr, 1974). This two-mechanism architecture is not a design flaw but an adaptive achievement: it evolved because no organism relying solely on unprocessed sensory input could successfully navigate perceptual ambiguity, finite cognitive resources, social complexity, or survival and reproductive pressures. The HOW and the WHY are inseparable — the specific mechanisms through which minds construct reality reflect the specific challenges that shaped them across evolutionary, developmental, and real-time timescales.
Perception as Construction: Closed Programs and Visual Ambiguity
The clearest evidence that cognition imposes structure rather than merely receiving it comes from visual perception. In the Rosenberg lecture on Visual Perception and Illusions, the visual system is presented not as a passive camera but as an inference engine that resolves inherently underdetermined input. The retinal image is ambiguous: the same two-dimensional projection can correspond to infinitely many three-dimensional configurations. The brain resolves this using top-down constraints — prior assumptions about light sources, surface properties, and object continuity — to produce a stable, useful percept (Rosenberg, "Visual Perception and Illusions," slide 12, [slide number flagged as approximate]).
Carbon (2014) documents how human-made illusions exploit precisely these constraints, revealing the inferential machinery by making it fail. Crucially, Gandhi et al. (2015) showed that individuals who gained sight after a lifetime of congenital blindness were immediately susceptible to certain visual illusions, suggesting that some perceptual construction mechanisms are species-typical closed programs — not learned through visual experience but built into the architecture of the visual system. This finding is theoretically important: it demonstrates that "going beyond" is not solely the product of individual learning. Some constructive biases are canalized by evolution because rapid, automatic perceptual resolution is adaptive. An organism that paused to consider all possible interpretations of an ambiguous scene before acting would not survive long. The closed program trades occasional error (the illusion) for consistent speed and efficiency — a bargain that natural selection has apparently endorsed across the species.
Attention and Memory: Open Programs Managing Finite Resources
While closed programs explain cross-individual convergence in constructed reality, open programs explain much of the variation — and both are necessary because the cognitive system faces severe resource constraints. The Awh lecture on the Limits of Conscious Awareness establishes that working memory holds approximately four chunks of information simultaneously (Luck & Vogel, 1997), and that even dramatic changes in a visual scene go undetected when attention is redirected (Simons & Levin, 1997). The world the conscious mind inhabits is not a faithful copy of the environment but a compressed, selectively attended reconstruction.
The mind's response to this bottleneck is constructive: it uses prior knowledge and expectation to generate a "good enough" model of the environment, inferring what is probably there rather than encoding everything. This is an open-program strategy because what counts as "probable" or "relevant" depends on learned context and individual experience. The HOW is attentional selection and schema-driven gap-filling; the WHY is that finite cognitive resources require prioritization. An organism that attempted to encode all available sensory detail would be paralyzed by the computational cost. Constructing a plausible model — going beyond the given — is not a limitation but a solution.
Concepts and Memory: Open Programs Shaping Categorization and Reconstruction
The open-program character of construction becomes especially clear when considering concepts and long-term memory. Henly's lecture on the Power of Concepts and Categorization draws on Medin (1989) to argue that categories are not simple feature lists but theory-laden structures that actively filter how new stimuli are perceived and interpreted. Archambault et al. (1999) demonstrate that conceptual priming can reshape perception itself — the same object is processed differently depending on the category into which it is placed. Here, learning shapes the constructive machinery: open programs built through experience determine which features are salient and what inferences are warranted.
Long-term memory extends this constructive logic across time. Gallo's lecture on Constructing True and False Realities from Long-term Memory presents memory not as a recording but as a reconstructive process driven by schemas and gist extraction. Roediger (1996) showed that people reliably "remember" words that were never presented when those words are semantically consistent with a studied list — a memory illusion that reveals the schema-driven, meaning-prioritizing architecture of recall. Murphy et al. (2019) showed that false memories for fake news can persist even after correction, because source monitoring — the process of attributing a memory to its origin — is itself an inferential process prone to error. The WHY is important here: a memory system optimized for gist and relational meaning, rather than verbatim storage, generalizes better to novel situations. The cost is susceptibility to false recall, but the benefit is a flexible, efficient knowledge base capable of supporting inference and transfer — an open-program advantage in a complex, ever-changing environment.
Social Construction and Evolutionary Bias: Programs Across Timescales
The constructive mind is not limited to physical perception and memory. Social cognition involves the same dynamic, and it is here that the interaction of timescales — evolutionary, developmental, and real-time — becomes most visible.
Keysar's lecture on Constructing Social Reality draws on Gilbert (2002) to describe a two-stage process: rapid, automatic dispositional attribution (a closed-program default) followed by effortful, often incomplete correction (an open-program override). The mind first categorizes and attributes — inferring intentions, traits, and mental states from minimal behavioral cues — and then, if resources and motivation permit, adjusts. Jones (1986) documented how expectancy effects propagate through interaction, confirming that social reality is not discovered but constructed and co-constructed. The closed-program defaults explain cross-cultural convergences in social inference; the open-program corrections explain individual and cultural divergence.
Bian's lecture on the Development of Social Categories introduces essentialism as a cognitive default that organizes social perception early in development. Rhodes and Mandalaywala (2017) show that children treat social categories as reflecting deep, natural properties — a constructive bias that simplifies the social environment by treating surface variation as expressive of underlying essences. Bian, Leslie, and Cimpian (2017) demonstrate that gender stereotypes constrain children's self-concept and aspirations by age six, well before much explicit instruction. The HOW involves category learning and essentialist inference; the WHY is that social categories reduce the complexity of predicting others' behavior, even at the cost of distortion and inequity.
The evolutionary basis for systematic directional biases in construction is addressed by Maestripieri's lecture on the Evolutionary Basis for Cognitive Biases. Haselton and Nettle's (2006) "paranoid optimist" model — also called error management theory — predicts that when the costs of false negatives and false positives are asymmetric, natural selection should favor a biased decision-maker over an unbiased one. Li et al. (2012) show that self-protection and mating motives modulate loss aversion in predictable directions, consistent with domain-specific evolved biases. The mind goes beyond the information given not randomly but in the direction that historically minimized fitness costs: over-detecting threats, over-inferring social hostility, under-weighting base rates when survival is at stake. These are closed-program biases at the evolutionary timescale — the mind arrives pre-tuned to err in the adaptive direction.
Goldin-Meadow's lecture on Changing Realities ties these timescales together using Mayr's (1974) distinction between closed and open behavior programs. Closed programs, shaped by phylogeny, provide the species-typical scaffold — the visual inference machinery, the essentialist social categories, the threat-detection biases. Open programs, shaped by ontogenetic experience and cultural transmission, allow elaboration and updating. Both are necessary: closed programs ensure that construction starts from an adaptive baseline; open programs ensure that construction can track the variable, culturally specific demands of actual environments. Tinbergen's (1968) questions about mechanism, development, function, and phylogeny map exactly onto this dual-program framework, and they remind us that no single timescale is sufficient to explain why the mind constructs the realities it does.
Conclusion
The mind goes beyond the information given because it must — and it does so through a two-mechanism architecture that reflects the challenges it evolved to face. Closed programs explain the species-typical convergences in constructed reality: the visual inference errors that appear at sight onset, the essentialist categories that emerge early in development, the threat-detection biases that tilt social inference. Open programs explain the individual and cultural divergences: the conceptual categories built through learning, the reconstructive memories shaped by prior knowledge, the social corrections that culture teaches. Together, they operate across milliseconds of perception, years of development, and millennia of evolutionary selection to produce a single outcome: an organism that constructs a workable, efficient, and often adaptive representation of a world it can never perceive in full. The HOW — closed and open programs interlocking across perceptual, conceptual, memorial, and social domains — is inseparable from the WHY — adaptive pressures at every timescale that made passive reception insufficient and active construction necessary. The mind constructs reality because that is what minds, shaped by millions of years of challenge, are built to do.
