
The Constructive Mind: How and Why We Go Beyond the Information Given
The phrase "the information given" refers to the raw sensory data our brains receive from the external world — the photons striking the retina, the sound waves entering the ear, the immediate inputs of experience. To "go beyond" this information means that the mind does not passively record reality like a camera; instead, it actively supplements, interprets, and reconstructs what is there, drawing on stored knowledge, expectations, and associations to produce a coherent experience. The central claim of this paper is that the mind systematically goes beyond the information given through both closed perceptual programs, which operate automatically through top-down processing, and open programs, which are shaped by individual experience and long-term memory — and that both serve the essential functional purpose of generating fast, coherent, and actionable representations of a world that is simply too complex and ambiguous for any system to process in full.
HOW: Top-Down Processing and the Constructed Percept
One of the clearest illustrations of how the mind goes beyond sensory input comes from the study of visual perception and illusions. Carbon (2014) argues that perception is not a passive registration of physical reality but rather an active, constructive process driven by "best guess heuristics." The perceptual system does not simply record what is there; it interprets, amplifies, and fills in. Carbon's treatment of the blind spot is instructive: where the optic nerve meets the retina, there are no photoreceptors, yet we perceive no gap in our visual field. The brain does not leave a hole — it intelligently reconstructs the missing information by analyzing the surrounding context, producing seamless experience out of genuinely absent data. This is going beyond the information given in the most literal sense: the brain invents perceptual content where none exists.
More broadly, Carbon (2014) describes how top-down processing — the influence of stored knowledge and expectations on incoming sensory signals — is the primary engine by which perception goes beyond the given. The man-rat bistable image illustrates this vividly: whether a viewer sees a man or a rodent depends heavily on what semantic concepts were recently activated in their mind. We perceive, in Carbon's words, "what we know." Prior exposure to the word "man" shifts interpretation of an ambiguous stimulus toward that category. Here the mind is not reading off a feature of the world; it is projecting a stored representation onto it. This mechanism — top-down activation shaping bottom-up input — recurs across all the perceptual phenomena Carbon discusses, from the Kanizsa triangle (where illusory contours are perceived with no physical basis) to simultaneous contrast effects (where the brain amplifies differences in luminance that do not objectively exist). In each case, the perceptual system generates structure that exceeds what the stimulus alone would license.
The Gallo lecture (slide 6) situates this within the broader framework of the course: "Top-down effects" are defined as "when representations stored in LTM guide or bias perception, attention, and understanding." This connection between long-term memory and moment-to-moment perception is crucial — it means that what we have previously experienced literally shapes what we currently see. The mind does not receive the world; it meets the world halfway, projecting its accumulated knowledge onto incoming signals.
HOW: The Limits of Conscious Awareness and Sparse Representation
A second mechanism by which the mind goes beyond the information given concerns the dramatic limits of conscious visual processing — limits that necessitate active construction rather than passive registration. Luck and Vogel (1997) demonstrated through sequential comparison tasks that visual working memory can retain information about only approximately four objects at a time, regardless of how many features those objects possess. When observers were shown arrays of colored squares, performance dropped sharply above set sizes of four, whether memory was tested for color, orientation, or both together. The mind, in other words, cannot hold a complete picture of the visual scene — it stores a dramatically sparse, object-based summary.
This finding connects directly to Simons and Levin's (1997) review of change blindness. Across a striking range of paradigms — changes made during saccades, during film cuts, during a blank flicker, and even in real-world face-to-face interactions — observers consistently failed to notice large, physically salient changes to visual scenes. In one real-world study, a person asking for directions failed to notice that their conversation partner had been completely replaced by a different individual while a door passed between them; only 50% of observers detected the substitution. The authors conclude that "relatively little visual information is preserved from one view to the next," and that what is stored is the gist of a scene — its abstract meaning — rather than precise visual detail (Simons & Levin, 1997).
This is a second, complementary form of going beyond the information given. Because the mind cannot store the full sensory array, it must use what it has — abstract schematic representations — to reconstruct experience across time. Between fixations, between glances, between film cuts, the brain assumes continuity where it has not confirmed it, filling gaps with expectations. As Simons and Levin (1997) argue, this inferential continuity may actually be what produces our phenomenal sense of a stable, continuous world: the system that replaces precise detail with gist-based assumption does not feel like a limitation, but like seamless experience.
HOW: Long-Term Memory and the Construction of False Realities
The constructive activity of the mind extends beyond perception into the retrieval of past events from long-term memory. The Gallo lecture (slides 12, 32) introduces the activation-monitoring framework for understanding why memory goes beyond what was experienced: the hippocampus automatically activates associated information when a cue is encountered, and the prefrontal cortex is responsible for monitoring these activations and distinguishing actually experienced events from merely associated ones. False memories arise, on this account, when activation of related information is strong but monitoring fails to correctly identify its source.
The DRM paradigm demonstrates this with unusual precision (Gallo lecture, slide 31). When participants study lists of semantically associated words (e.g., sour, candy, sugar, bitter, taste, tooth) and are later asked about the word sweet, they frequently "remember" seeing it — with high confidence — even though it never appeared. The semantic activation created by the list items spreads automatically to the category prototype, and that activation, absent adequate source monitoring, is experienced as genuine recollection. This is the mind going beyond the information given in long-term memory: constructing a past that was never encoded, from the materials of semantic association.
The imagination inflation studies cited in the Gallo lecture (slides 51–61) extend this further. Wade, Garry, Read, and Lindsay (2002) showed that when participants were shown a doctored childhood photograph placing them in a hot air balloon they had never ridden and were encouraged across multiple interviews to "try to recall or imagine" the event, half eventually produced detailed false memories with subjective confidence. Mental imagery activates the same cortical regions as actual perception (Gallo lecture, slide 52), which means that imagined experience can leave traces indistinguishable, to the monitoring system, from genuine perception. The mind cannot reliably tell the difference between what it experienced and what it vividly imagined — and so it goes beyond the experienced past to construct a plausible one.
WHY: The Functional Logic of Constructive Minds
Having established several mechanisms through which the mind goes beyond the information given, we must now ask why. What functional pressures — evolutionary and developmental — explain why minds work this way rather than more accurately?
Carbon (2014) offers the most direct answer for the case of perception. The perceptual system's primary task is not fidelity but speed and actionability: the organism must assess the core meaning of a complex scene rapidly enough to respond appropriately. A system that attempted to register every feature of every object in a scene would be overwhelmed — paralyzed by the "big data" of raw sensory input. Instead, top-down processes compress this data into schematized, prototype-matching representations that can be acted upon immediately. The illusions that this system produces — the simultaneous contrast effect, the Kanizsa triangle, the bistable figures — are not bugs but byproducts of its adaptive design. Carbon explicitly reframes illusions not as failures but as evidence of the system's "incredible, highly complex and efficient capabilities." The perceptual mind goes beyond the information given because no biological system could survive by waiting for complete information.
The same logic applies to change blindness. Simons and Levin (1997) note, drawing on William James, that a system too precisely tracking every visual detail would present a "blooming, buzzing confusion" — the very richness of a busy city scene, with its constant motion and occlusion and shifting detail, would be cognitively catastrophic if every change triggered a change-detection signal. A system that represents only the gist and assumes continuity of detail is better calibrated to the statistical structure of the natural environment, where most changes are irrelevant and the meaningful structure of scenes persists across time.
The memory case is more complex, but the logic is similar. The activation mechanism that generates false memories is the same mechanism that allows productive inference, generalization, and meaning-making. The hippocampal system that automatically activates associates and prototypes is what makes it possible to understand the meaning of a new event, to categorize it, to integrate it with existing knowledge. The prefrontal monitoring system that attempts to distinguish real from associated is effortful and fallible — but a memory system without automatic spreading activation would be one that could not learn or generalize at all. False memories are the cost of a system designed for flexible inference rather than perfect recording.
The Commonality and Variability of Constructed Reality
A final question worth addressing is how much our constructed realities overlap. The course prompt asks whether individuals share the same reality. The evidence from these readings suggests a partial answer: closed perceptual programs, shaped by evolutionary history and shared neural architecture, produce broadly similar constructions across individuals — most people see the Kanizsa triangle, most people are change-blind, most people have a visual working memory capacity of approximately four objects. These are species-typical features. But open programs, shaped by individual experience and cultural learning, introduce systematic individual differences. Carbon (2014) notes that even "objectivity" in perception — defined as consensus among beholders — breaks down across individuals with different histories, different semantic networks, different priors. The Gallo lecture (slide 6) makes explicit that LTM-based top-down effects mean that individuals' different learning histories produce different perceptual and memorial constructions. The baseline architecture is shared; the contents are individual.
Conclusion
The mind goes beyond the information given because it must. The world is too complex, the data too vast, the time too short, for any biological system to register experience faithfully. Instead, the mind deploys stored knowledge to amplify and interpret ambiguous sensory signals, retains only gist-level representations across time, and reconstructs past events from the probabilistic traces of activation. These mechanisms — top-down perceptual construction, sparse working memory and change blindness, and activation-based false memory — each represent the same fundamental trade-off: fidelity for speed, completeness for actionability, accuracy for coherence. The constructive mind is not a broken camera; it is a meaning-making system optimally adapted to a world where good-enough and fast is nearly always better than perfect and slow.
